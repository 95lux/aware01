\subsection{Digital Audio Fundamentals}
\label{sec:digital-audio-fundamentals}

\subsubsection{Sampling}

A continuous-time audio signal $x_c(t)$ is converted to a discrete-time sequence by sampling it at regular intervals of period $T = 1/f_s$:

\[
x[n] = x_c(nT)
\]

The \textit{Nyquist--Shannon sampling theorem} states that a bandlimited signal with highest frequency component $f_{\max}$ can be perfectly reconstructed from its samples provided the sample rate satisfies $f_s \geq 2 f_{\max}$ \cite[p.~523]{oppenheimDiscretetimeSignalProcessing2014}.
The frequency $f_s / 2$ is called the \textit{Nyquist frequency} and represents the highest frequency representable at a given sample rate.

If signal energy is present above the Nyquist frequency at the time of sampling, those components fold back into the audible band as \textit{aliasing} --- a form of distortion that cannot be removed after the fact.
A lowpass \textit{anti-aliasing filter} applied before the ADC suppresses out-of-band energy to prevent this.

This project uses a sample rate of \qty{48}{kHz}, yielding a Nyquist frequency of \qty{24}{kHz}, which covers the full range of human hearing.

\subsubsection{Quantization}

Each sample amplitude is represented as an integer with $N$ bits, dividing the signal range into $2^N$ discrete levels.
The rounding error introduced by this process is \textit{quantization noise}, which limits the dynamic range of the system.
For uniform quantization, the theoretical signal-to-noise ratio is approximately \cite[p.~175]{zoelzer_dafx}:

\[
\text{SNR} \approx 6.02 \cdot N + 1.76 \; \text{dB}
\]

A 16-bit system therefore provides approximately \qty{98}{dB} of dynamic range, which is sufficient for audio applications.
The TLV320AIC3204 codec used in this project operates at 16-bit resolution over I\textsuperscript{2}S.
